# Editorial Style Wiki

## What works (keep doing)
- Stay tightly inside the digest and build the piece around the best-supported hook rather than reaching for extra color.
- Center the story on one clear analytical thread when several facts are available, especially roster-building or schedule-tradeoff angles.
- Use concrete figures and schedule details directly in the reporting; specific numbers make the note feel grounded.
- Keep the piece focused on verified implications without drifting into unsupported outlook language.
- Let a thin or constrained update remain narrow if the sourcing is strong and the execution is clean.
- Make the headline reflect the core premise plainly instead of overselling the story.
- Keep persona work aligned with the angle; [analyst] should foreground construction, balance, and trade-offs.
- Avoid meta-language and let the sourced facts carry the frame.
- When the source is genuinely sparse, stay minimal and factual rather than forcing a fuller narrative.
- Keep strong grounding and specificity visible in the lede, not buried later.

## What to avoid
- Don’t stretch a limited set of digest facts into a broader takeaway than the evidence supports.
- Don’t add unsupported color, context, or narrative momentum when the digest already provide enough to report cleanly.
- Don’t drift into season outlook or other speculative framing unless the source explicitly supports it.
- Don’t bury the main supported hook under extra framing or explanatory padding.
- Don’t let a headline promise more significance than the body can actually prove.
- Don’t widen a narrow update into a more comprehensive story just to create drama.
- Don’t rely on generic setup language when a direct, sourced statement will do.
- Don’t treat sparse sourcing as permission to infer implications that aren’t spelled out.
- Don’t use meta or source-aware phrasing when straightforward reporting is available.
- [analyst] Don’t soften the core numbers or trade-offs into vague narrative; keep the lede on the concrete roster/schedule facts.
- In dismisses, if the digest has no usable facts, treat it as a source-side problem, not a prose-fixable one.
- Don’t force team-context or other irrelevant framing onto a story when the digest doesn’t support it.
