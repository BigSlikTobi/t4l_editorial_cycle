# Editorial Style Wiki

## What works (keep doing)
- Lead with the one confirmed development in a clean, direct sentence; thin reports can work when the first line is decisive.
- Keep headline and lede pointed at the same concrete fact, but vary the wording so they don’t feel duplicated.
- Center short insider pieces on the verified transaction, quote, or team message when that is the strongest hook.
- Stay tightly inside the source digest when facts are limited; restrained reporting reads stronger than padded context.
- Let the confirmed event do the work instead of layering on implications the source never states.
- Use exact status, fit, or role language when the source supports it, especially for scheme, signing, cut, recovery, trial timing, or lineup fit.
- Clean news framing is stronger than source-meta or narrational phrasing in short insider pieces.
- Strong grounding can offset limited specificity when the execution stays disciplined.
- [analyst] Center the angle on the supported development and keep surrounding framing minimal.
- [columnist] A clear, source-supported hook can make a small update readable, but only if it stays direct and doesn’t overclaim.

## What to avoid
- Don’t make the headline, sub-head, lede, and body all repeat the same claim; repetition flattens the piece.
- Don’t widen one confirmed fit or transaction into a settled roster, depth-chart, or pecking-order story without evidence.
- Don’t invent roster logic, usage, or role conclusions from a thin digest.
- Don’t ask the reader to infer details the source does not provide, like terms, timing, or broader implications.
- Don’t let source narration or meta language crowd out the actual fact or quote.
- Don’t pad a thin source note with unsupported implications or extra context just to create depth.
- Don’t introduce broad significance unless the source actually supports it.
- Don’t turn one supported quote or transaction into a bigger team-wide claim unless the source backs that leap.
- Don’t treat a thin digest like a fuller reporting package; the rewrite should get sharper, not busier.
- Don’t build on an empty digest or placeholder source; if there are no usable facts to verify, the story is source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
- [columnist] Don’t replace a clear angle with vague phrasing; keep the lede useful and specific.
- [columnist] Don’t lean on unsupported spring-role or “keeping him happy” framing when the source material doesn’t substantiate it.
