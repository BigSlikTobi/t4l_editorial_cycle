# Editorial Style Wiki

## What works (keep doing)
- Lead with the single confirmed development in a direct sentence; a thin update can still work when the first line is unmistakable.
- Keep headline, lede, and subhead pointed at the same concrete fact without extra framing.
- Stay tightly inside the digest when facts are limited; disciplined restraint reads stronger than padded context.
- Let the confirmed event or quote do the work instead of layering on implications the source never states.
- Use exact procedural language when the source supports it, especially for trial stage, testimony, deliberations, or verdict status.
- Strong grounding can offset limited specificity when the execution stays disciplined.
- Useful context is welcome when it is sourced and does not drift into speculation.
- [analyst] Center the angle on supported facts and keep surrounding framing minimal when the digest is thin.
- [insider] Put the concrete roster or legal development in the headline and first sentence before any broader theory or roundup framing.
- [insider] A clear single-event update can carry a thin piece if headline, lede, and body all stay anchored to the same verified fact.
- [columnist] A short, direct quote can carry the story if the headline and intro stay tightly focused on that quote-driven angle.

## What to avoid
- Don’t make the headline, lede, and body all repeat the same claim; repetition flattens the piece.
- Don’t add explanatory language about what a procedural step means unless the source explicitly says so.
- Don’t imply a verdict, outcome, or next-step certainty when the digest only reports an in-process development.
- Don’t widen one verified update into a broader case-resolution story without evidence.
- Don’t introduce roundup/meta framing that is not present in the digest; it reads like fabricated connective tissue.
- Don’t ask the reader to infer details the source does not provide, like broader implications or likely results.
- Don’t let source narration or meta language crowd out the actual fact or quote.
- Don’t build the piece around unsupported digest-talk instead of source facts.
- Don’t assert a status or other milestone unless the provided digest clearly supports it.
- Don’t pad a thin source note with unsupported implications or extra context just to create depth.
- [insider] Don’t hang the lead on roundup or case-closure framing when the digest only supports one confirmed development.
- [insider] Don’t add case details, timing, or closure language that isn’t in the digest.
- [columnist] Don’t hang the lead on broad team framing when the source only supports a narrow, single-quote message.
- [columnist] Don’t try to salvage a story when the source digest has no usable facts beyond a headline-level premise; that’s source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
- [analyst] Don’t add unsupported market, cap, or shopping dynamics, and don’t introduce names that aren’t grounded in the source facts; if the source is effectively empty, the problem is source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
- [insider] Don’t use banned source-digest narration or meta phrases like “according to the source digest”; if the digest is empty, the problem is source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
- [columnist] Don’t build on rumor-only premises when the digest contains no verifiable facts to support the framing; that’s source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
