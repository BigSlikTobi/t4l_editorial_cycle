# Editorial Style Wiki

## What works (keep doing)
- Lead with the one confirmed news item when the digest is thin; a tight headline-to-lede match beats expanding the frame.
- Keep the headline and intro anchored to the same verified development so the reader gets the update immediately.
- Use the reported term and money only as far as the source supports; the concrete deal details can carry the story without extra framing.
- Keep the lede tightly source-backed and direct; clean, factual phrasing is stronger than adding interpretive context.
- Let a single verified transaction or agreement stand on its own when that is the only durable fact in the digest.
- Surface the core action immediately, then stop unless the digest clearly adds more.
- When the evidence is sparse, brevity can be the right form of discipline.
- Maintain a crisp insider-style update when the report is solid but limited; clarity matters more than breadth.
- [insider] Focus on the confirmed agreement, reported term, reported money, and destination/team connection rather than speculative implications.
- [insider] Tight, specific contract facts can fully carry the piece when they’re the only durable details available.

## What to avoid
- Don’t add unsupported claims about what the contract means for the team, player, or future strategy unless the digest explicitly supports them.
- Don’t widen a headline-level report into a broader narrative when the closed-world evidence is thin.
- Don’t make factual claims that go beyond the source digest; grounding failures are publish-stoppers.
- Don’t repeat the lede or create a mild loop by restating the same news in slightly different words.
- Don’t bury the core agreement under timeline, context, or significance language that isn’t directly sourced.
- Don’t treat a reported deal as a platform for extra certainty about downstream effects unless those effects are verified.
- Don’t overbuild a sparse digest into a polished story; if the support isn’t there, stay tightly factual.
- Don’t pad the intro with general commentary when one confirmed fact can do the job.
- [insider] Don’t infer team strategy, contract impact, or timing specifics beyond what the report directly confirms.
- If the factual base is effectively empty beyond the headline, keep the piece minimal rather than stretching it; that’s a source-limit issue, not a prose fix (source-side, not fixable in prose).
