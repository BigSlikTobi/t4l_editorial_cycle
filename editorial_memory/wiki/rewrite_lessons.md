# Editorial Style Wiki

## What works (keep doing)
- Lead with the single verified development in a clean, direct sentence; thin updates can work when the first line is decisive.
- Keep headline and lede pointed at the same concrete fact without adding extra explanatory framing.
- Center short insider updates on the one source-supported procedural change when that is the strongest hook.
- Stay tightly inside the digest when facts are limited; restrained reporting reads stronger than padded context.
- Let the confirmed event do the work instead of layering on implications the source never states.
- Use exact procedural language when the source supports it, especially for trial stage, testimony, deliberations, or verdict status.
- Strong grounding can offset limited specificity when the execution stays disciplined.
- Useful context is welcome when it stays sourced and doesn’t drift into speculation.
- [analyst] Center the angle on the supported facts and keep surrounding framing minimal when the digest is thin.
- [insider] Put the concrete visit/signing development in the headline and first sentence before any broader roster theory.
- [insider] A clear roster/secondary development can carry a thin piece if the headline, lede, and body all stay anchored to the same verified facts.

## What to avoid
- Don’t make the headline, lede, and body all repeat the same claim; repetition flattens the piece.
- Don’t add explanatory language about what a procedural step means unless the source explicitly says so.
- Don’t imply a verdict, outcome, or next-step certainty when the digest only reports deliberations or another in-process stage.
- Don’t widen one verified update into a broader case-resolution story without evidence.
- Don’t invent team-specific or other entity-specific framing when it isn’t part of the digest.
- Don’t ask the reader to infer details the source does not provide, like broader implications or likely results.
- Don’t let source narration or meta language crowd out the actual fact or quote.
- Don’t build the piece around unsupported digest-talk instead of source facts.
- Don’t assert a status or other milestone unless the provided digest clearly supports it.
- Don’t pad a thin source note with unsupported implications or extra context just to create depth.
- Don’t treat a thin digest like a fuller reporting package; the rewrite should get sharper, not busier.
- [analyst] Don’t add unsupported market, cap, or shopping dynamics, and don’t introduce names that aren’t grounded in the source facts; if the source is effectively empty, the problem is source-side, not fixable in prose (source-side, not fixable in prose).
